# CISO and Board Governance Requirements

**Reference:** CBN Risk-Based Cybersecurity Framework, Section on Governance
**Applies To:** Commercial banks, merchant banks, non-interest banks, and Payment Service Banks
**Prepared by:** TrustGrid Technology

---

## Why This Matters

Governance is the section of the CBN framework most likely to be treated as a formality and least likely to survive real scrutiny. A CISO title without genuine authority, independence, and board access does not satisfy the framework's intent.

---

## The CISO Requirement

The framework requires a named Chief Information Security Officer. This is not a box-ticking exercise. The CISO must have:

| Requirement | What It Means in Practice |
|---|---|
| Direct board or board-committee reporting line | The CISO briefs the Board or a Board risk committee directly, not through IT leadership |
| Independence from IT operations | The person responsible for security oversight should not also be responsible for IT service delivery, to avoid marking their own work |
| Sufficient authority | The CISO can require remediation of identified risks, not merely recommend it |
| Adequate resourcing | The CISO has budget and staff sufficient to execute the cybersecurity programme, not just a title |

### Common Gap

Many smaller institutions appoint an existing IT Head as CISO without adjusting the reporting line or resolving the conflict of interest. This satisfies the letter of "a named CISO exists" but not the framework's intent of independent oversight.

---

## Board-Level Governance

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| Board-approved cybersecurity policy | Must be reviewed and approved at Board level, not just by management |
| Defined risk appetite | The Board must articulate what level of cybersecurity risk is acceptable, in terms the Board actually understands |
| Regular reporting | Cybersecurity risk should be a standing item, not an annual briefing |
| Reporting in business terms | Reports to the Board should translate technical risk into financial, operational, and reputational impact |

### What Good Board Reporting Looks Like

Instead of: "We patched 47 vulnerabilities this quarter."

Better: "We reduced our critical vulnerability exposure by 60% this quarter, addressing risks that could have resulted in service disruption affecting approximately 200,000 customers."

The framework expects the Board to genuinely understand and own cybersecurity risk, not simply receive a technical status update they nod through.

---

## Governance Self-Assessment Questions

Use these questions in your internal audit or governance review:

1. If the CISO identified a critical risk requiring significant budget, could they escalate it directly to the Board without going through IT leadership first?
2. Has the Board ever pushed back on a cybersecurity recommendation, or has every report simply been accepted without discussion?
3. Does the CISO have a documented budget separate from general IT spend?
4. When was cybersecurity risk last discussed as a standalone Board agenda item, not bundled into a broader IT or risk update?
5. Could a new Board member, unfamiliar with your institution, understand your cybersecurity risk exposure from your last Board report alone?

If the honest answer to any of these is no, that is a governance gap under this framework, regardless of whether a CISO title technically exists.
